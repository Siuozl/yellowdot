# Why Some Nations Are Dying Quickly

## My First Experience With a Clan

So I thought leading a clan would be easy, as many people had one with a decent number of members. My clan was **TCL**, which was inspired by an Indian YouTuber called Fleet — TCL stands for **"The Crimson Lilyville."** I was pretty proud of what I made, but no one was ready to join. Here are some reasons why they didn't want to:

### How to Actually Get People to Join

1. **Be well-known in the community.** To make a clan and actually get people to join, you have to be recognized.
2. **Be strong.** That means being able to beat at least a fair number of people in the game.
3. **Be rich.** Rich means more than **10M in-game currency** — don't look broke.
4. **Give them shelter.** This is essential for attracting members.
5. **Protect them.** This is the most important one — it builds **trust and honesty**.

> *The information above is about getting as many players as possible, and what to avoid so your clan doesn't lose members.*

## Why People Often Lose Players

*Why aren't smaller nations like EE, Imperials, IOM, etc.?*

1. People often give up when there's even a small chance of failure — like how *"99% of gamblers leave before they win big."* (Just an air quote.)
2. People don't do the things listed above and give up too quickly.
3. Many clan leaders are good at bending and teach their guild members — when that happens, people join fast.
4. Many clans have a large number of mines where they can find ores easily.
5. Last but not least, some people offer money for joining — like how Indian politics works. Don't mind that, just keep reading.

## What's Really Happening on the Server

Lately, a lot of nations have been dying quickly. At first they start strong — many players join, everyone seems active and excited — but after a few days, things begin to fall apart. Some players lose interest and stop logging in, while others leave to join stronger nations like the **Imperials** or **Elemental Empire**, which makes smaller nations even weaker.

From what I've seen, internal problems also play a big role. Leaders argue with members, trust breaks, and sometimes people even betray their own team. Bad leadership is another major reason — without proper plans or goals, members get bored and slowly drift away. Grinding also becomes too much for some players, as building and maintaining a base takes time and effort, and not everyone wants to keep doing that. In the end, many nations are created too quickly without planning, and they disappear just as fast, making the server feel constantly changing and unpredictable.

## 25 Reasons Nations Die — And How to Fix Them

### Part 1

1. **Players lose interest quickly**
   — Keep members engaged by doing events, fights, or group activities so they stay active.

2. **Stronger nations dominate smaller ones**
   — Form alliances with other small nations, or focus on smart strategies instead of direct fights.

3. **Internal fights and betrayal**
   — Build trust, choose members carefully, and keep communication clear inside the nation.

4. **Bad leadership**
   — Leaders should plan goals, guide members, and stay active to keep the nation alive.

5. **Players keep switching nations**
   — Reward loyalty and make members feel important so they don't leave easily.

6. **Grinding becomes too tiring**
   — Divide work between members so no one feels overworked.

7. **Nations are created without planning**
   — Think about goals, structure, and roles before starting a nation.

8. **Poor communication**
   — Use Discord or chat properly and keep everyone updated.

9. **No events or goals**
   — Organize regular wars, training, or fun events to keep things interesting.

10. **Players join only for benefits and leave**
    — Recruit loyal players and avoid trusting random players too quickly.

11. **No clear roles in the nation**
    — Assign roles like leader, fighter, builder, and recruiter so everyone knows what to do.

12. **Inactivity of leaders**
    — Leaders should log in daily, or assign co-leaders to manage things when they're offline.

13. **Weak base defense**
    — Build hidden or strong bases and improve defenses to survive raids.

14. **New players not trained properly**
    — Teach members combat and bending skills so they become useful in fights.

15. **Too much ego between members**
    — Keep things chill and remind everyone it's a team, not a solo game.

16. **Lack of recruitment**
    — Actively recruit players instead of waiting, and look for active members.

17. **No backup plan after losing a war**
    — Always have reserve resources and a second base ready.

18. **Members not communicating during fights**
    — Use voice chat or quick coordination so fights are more organized.

19. **Focusing only on fighting**
    — Balance between fighting, building, and grinding to grow properly.

20. **Copying other nations blindly**
    — Create your own style and strategy instead of copying others.

21. **Toxic environment inside the nation**
    — Remove toxic players early and keep the environment friendly.

22. **Poor time coordination**
    — Plan activities when most members are online.

23. **Overexpanding too fast**
    — Grow slowly and make sure current members are stable before adding more.

24. **Trusting enemies too easily**
    — Be careful with alliances and always stay alert.

25. **No long-term vision**
    — Set future goals like becoming a top nation or dominating certain areas.

## Deeper Observations

Many players join a nation with excitement on the first day — they help build, fight, and stay active — but after a day or two they slowly stop logging in, the chat goes silent, and the nation starts to feel empty.

Another thing I've noticed is that many nations start without a clear plan. At first it may not seem like a big issue, but over time it creates confusion among members. Some players focus on building while others want to fight, and some just wander around without doing anything. This lack of direction slowly makes the nation feel unorganized, and people begin to lose interest because they don't know what they're working towards.

There is also the problem of inactive communication — even if players are online, they're not really talking or coordinating. The chat feels empty and silent, which makes the nation feel dead even when it isn't completely inactive. From what I've seen, active communication is what keeps a nation alive, and without it members feel disconnected and eventually leave.

Another major issue is that some nations rely too much on a few strong players. When those players go offline or leave, the entire nation struggles to function. This creates an imbalance where new or weaker players don't feel confident enough to step up, and the nation becomes dependent on a small group — which is not sustainable in the long run.

I've also noticed that many players join nations expecting quick success or rewards, but when things don't go as expected they leave almost immediately. This mindset makes it difficult for nations to grow, because building something strong takes time and patience, and not everyone is willing to put in that effort.

In some cases, nations fall apart simply because they try to expand too quickly. They recruit too many players without properly managing them, which leads to chaos and lack of coordination. Instead of growing stronger, the nation becomes harder to control and slowly breaks down from within.

Another interesting thing is how fear plays a role. Smaller nations often avoid taking risks because they're afraid of stronger ones, and this limits their growth. They stay hidden, avoid fights, and never really improve — which eventually leads to boredom and inactivity among members.

Finally, it feels like the overall mindset of players has changed. Earlier, people used to focus more on teamwork and long-term growth, but now many players are focused on quick gains and personal progress. As teamwork becomes weaker, the chances of a nation's survival also decrease.

---

Something else that stands out is how many nations struggle with **consistency**. One day the nation feels alive — players are talking, moving around, doing things together — but the next day it feels completely empty. This kind of inconsistency makes it hard for members to stay motivated, because they don't know what to expect when they log in. Over time, they stop checking in at all.

I've also noticed that some nations focus too much on **short-term goals** instead of thinking about the future. They might win a fight or gain some resources, but they don't plan what comes next. Because of that, their progress doesn't last long — after a while, everything they built starts to fall apart, and it feels like all their effort went to waste.

Another problem is that many members **don't feel valued** inside their nation. When players feel like they're just another number and not important to the team, they slowly lose interest and stop contributing. Nations that actually appreciate their members and involve them in decisions tend to last much longer.

There are also situations where nations become **too serious** and forget that the game is supposed to be fun. Everything turns into pressure — whether it's grinding, fighting, or defending — and this can quickly burn out players, especially newer ones who just wanted to enjoy the experience. Once the fun is gone, players don't see a reason to stay.

In some cases, **poor decision-making during important moments** can destroy a nation. For example, choosing to attack at the wrong time, trusting the wrong players, or even revealing their base location by mistake. These small decisions might not seem big at first, but they can have long-term consequences that the nation cannot recover from.

Another thing that affects nations is the **lack of adaptation**. The server keeps changing — new players arrive, new strategies emerge, different playstyles develop — but some nations keep doing the same things without improving. Because of that, they fall behind others who are constantly learning and evolving.

It's also common to see nations **lose momentum after a big loss**, such as losing a war or getting raided. After that happens, many members feel discouraged, and instead of rebuilding, they slowly give up. This loss of morale is one of the biggest reasons why nations disappear completely.

Some nations also struggle because they **try to control everything too strictly**. Leaders might limit what members can do or not trust them enough, which creates frustration. Instead of feeling like a team, it starts to feel restrictive, and members eventually leave to find more freedom elsewhere.

Another interesting issue is how **rumors and misunderstandings** can damage a nation even without direct conflict. Sometimes false information spreads between players and creates unnecessary tension inside the group, and before they realize it, the trust between members is already broken.

Finally, it feels like **timing** plays a big role in whether a nation survives or not. If a nation is created at the wrong time — when most players are inactive or busy — it struggles to grow from the start. Even if the idea is good, it doesn't get the attention it needs to succeed.

---

Another point that is often ignored is how a **lack of identity** affects a nation. Many nations don't have a clear style or purpose — they don't stand out in any way, and because of that, players don't feel attached to them. When a nation has no identity, it becomes forgettable, and members don't feel proud to be part of it, which slowly leads to its downfall.

I've also seen that some nations fail because they **rely too much on luck** rather than effort. They hope for easy wins or random success instead of consistently working towards improvement, and when things don't go their way, they don't have the strength or preparation to recover.

Another issue is that **players sometimes join nations without really understanding how things work**. They might not know the rules, the expectations, or the goals of the nation. This creates confusion and mistakes, which can frustrate both the player and the leader. Over time, this lack of understanding leads to disconnection and inactivity.

There is also the problem of **comparison**, where smaller nations constantly measure themselves against bigger and stronger ones. This creates a feeling that they're not good enough. Instead of focusing on their own growth, they feel discouraged and lose motivation, which affects their progress even more.

In some situations, **nations collapse simply because they lose their original purpose**. What started as a group of friends or a strong idea slowly turns into something unclear, and without that original motivation, members don't feel the same connection anymore and begin to drift away.

Another thing I noticed is that **some players are only active during specific times**, and when those times don't match with the rest of the nation, it creates a disconnect. Even if the nation has active players, they're never online together, which makes teamwork almost impossible and gives the feeling that the nation is inactive.

And sometimes it really just comes down to **patience**. Many players expect fast results, but building a strong nation takes time, effort, and consistency. Those who aren't willing to wait and grow slowly end up leaving early, which weakens the nation before it even has a chance to succeed.

Finally, after looking at all these reasons, it becomes clear that **nations are not just dying because of one single problem, but because of many small issues adding up over time** — from inactivity and poor leadership to lack of communication and patience. Everything plays a role, and if these problems are not handled properly, even the strongest-looking nation can fall apart.

Another thing people don't really think about is how silence would feel. Right now there is always some kind of noise around us — notifications, reels playing in the background, random messages popping up. If phones suddenly disappeared, that constant noise would be gone. At first it might feel peaceful, but after a while it could start to feel strange, almost uncomfortable. People are so used to distraction that being alone with their thoughts might actually feel difficult.

You would probably notice people looking around more instead of staring down at a screen. They would actually observe what is happening around them — the way people talk, small details in places they pass every day, even their own thoughts. Conversations might become deeper too, because nothing is pulling attention away every few seconds.

At the same time, boredom would increase — and that's not necessarily a bad thing. Boredom can push people to be creative, to try new things, or to reconnect with hobbies they forgot about. Things like drawing, writing, or even just sitting and thinking could slowly come back into people's lives.

In a weird way, losing phones for a week would not just change routines — it might actually change how people see the world around them.
